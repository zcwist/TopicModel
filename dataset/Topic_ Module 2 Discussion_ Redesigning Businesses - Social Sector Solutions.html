        Before beginning this inquiry, an organization I am working with had already identified a question: “How might we reach out and engage people to become supporters of our organization?” At least, this was the question that seemed implied by the tasks that they were looking to have done. And indeed, this organization is looking to turn some aspects of its business in a totally different direction. For this exercise, I will examine the underlying assumptions about the question, and see what can be learned if I come up with a different question.  The organization I am evaluating is an environmental advocacy nonprofit focused on protecting water here in the Bay Area. Its core belief about creating value is that by stopping pollution and creating better policies around water protection, everyone in the region benefits, in particular those who have an appreciation for clean water in the region or who use it for recreation.  Some underlying beliefs include:  - protecting public goods such as water quality in the bay, rivers, etc., is the morally right thing to do, and people generally agree with this; - companies that pollute water respond to pressure from NGOs and civil society to adopt cleaner practices; - most people want to support causes even if  they “only” indirectly benefit from them; - generating widespread knowledge about the organization will lead to greater support; - better understanding who current supporters are will lead to better future outreach;  - people who have supported the organization currently or in the past are a good indicator of who their most promising supporters are in the future; - local buy in is important for continued legitimacy of the organization’s work. The second-to-last belief, that people who have supported in the past are a good indicator of who may support them in the future, is the one I will dissect and turn on its head to see how it changes the question.  In fact, this underlying belief immediately shifts the “how might we?” question. If we instead consider that existing supporters might not be indicative of the most active or numerous supporters in the future, the immediate question becomes instead, “hmw discover who the most promising, active, and numerous potential supporters of our organization are?” Where do the interests of the most potentially active groups in our community and the goals of our organization intersect? How can those be best communicated and acted upon?  Though I preferred the presentation and relative simplicity of the business model canvas, in this case, the impact canvas is more appropriate. In this case, the fundamental purpose of the organization does not change: key partners, key activities, and stakeholders stay basically the same. However, from a marketing perspective, the key resources, value propositions, channels, costs, and revenue streams could all be affected substantially. Among these, key resources, value propositions, and channel are the most critical to re-evaluate. They key resources are the aspects of the organization itself - what is it that it already does that could speak to this potential audience? How can the value proposition be more actively and efficiently be communicated - and how (through what channels) should support be encouraged? In this case, these answers are a mystery - they are not just based on doing more of the same of what they had already been doing to attract their existing supporters.

              The US education system was built on the belief that a standard set of skills, competencies, and experiences would adequately prepare students for work and life. As technology, culture, and society have evolved the core idea that there are one size fits all "best practice" for education has remained entrenched. Although there are innovators today (some public and charter schools, edtech companies, etc.) who are pushing the boundaries of how we prepare today's youth, the majority of students are still not being adequately prepared to lead healthy, successful lives.  Some core beliefs preventing more significant change are:  Trained educators know what is best for students and communities Our education system is doing what it was designed to do (debatable whether this is true and, if it is, what those goals are) Changing the education system would be too hard There is a clearly definable set of skills and knowledge students need In my experience, a lot of ed reform centers on, "how might we redesign schools?" The idea is that schools must be redesigned, but I think the real issue runs deeper. Instead we should be answering, "how might we reimagine how students think?" I was raised in an environment where you learned from books, did homework, and paid attention to what teachers said. Mastering those competencies allowed me to get to Haas, but they're outdated if we're trying to prepare students to be successful in a fast-moving and ever-changing modern world. Even if schools (class structures, content, curriculum) are redesigned to be more fun, engaging, etc., our students will continue to fail if we don't find a way to prepare them better as learners. Today's education system can create excellent students, but it rarely emphasizes curiosity, innovation, risk-taking, and open-mindedness. Rather than rethinking schools so they better prepare students to find right answers, I think we should be exploring how to prepare students to ask the right questions, consider new possibilities, and focus on learning.

While the trust issues that arise in any sharing economy would apply here – no one wants food that’s unappealing or, worse, unsafe – I think our growing familiarity with ratings- and reviews-based credibility, along with the success of meal-kit delivery services like Blue Apron, may have paved a path for creative food solutions that rethink food waste as opportunity. What do others think?

The most important long-held belief is that students can and should finish high school in 4 years. The idea of a student remaining in high school past the age of 18 doesn’t make sense to people on a number of levels. First, 18 year-olds are seen as adults, not kids; they can’t be forced to go anywhere each day, and we tend to believe that they should be gaining independence and work experience (or preparation for work experience through college). Second, schools’ budgets and operations depend on their ability to predict the number of students they will have each year. If students did not progress predictably from grade to grade, schools would be faced with massive logistical challenges in predicting their budgets and in creating the right course offerings and slotting students in appropriately. Turning this belief on its head leads us to ask: How might we redesign the high school experience so that students progress based on the knowledge and skills needed for their chosen careers, rather than their time spent in the building?
