    Part of innovation is taking what you experience, including what you read, and thinking about the missing pieces, the next steps, or just something you are curious about. In each module of PFPS, you will do so with one or more of the readings from that week by considering the application of the concepts in the readings to your own life and work, capturing your thoughts on the Discussions pages in bCourses and responding to your classmates’ posts.

    This week’s introductory readings ask you to consider “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up” (aka hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship) frameworks, the basic mindsets and capabilities that underlie them and how they might play out in your lives or the organizations you work in.  As you read, think about the parallels and differences among the three approaches to innovation.

    Compose a thoughtful answer to the questions: what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”?  For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?  In what ways have the organizations you've worked in supported the development of these skills or mindsets? In what ways might they better support these approaches? Which approaches are likely to be most valuable in the Applied Innovation course you are taking?  What questions do you have about the approaches that you would like to discuss with your classmates?  Be specific in your responses.  

    Post your response, including questions for your classmates, on bCourses and then respond to at least two of your classmates.

    General Guidelines for Discussion: Be thoughtful, constructive and inclusive.  In your responses, be kind, present objections or different points of view open to the possibility that there may be a reasonable response, and object to the responses rather than to the people.  Objections are fine, but you can also choose to be constructive, and you needn't repeat the same objection multiple times.  Be inclusive in your contributions, acknowledging previous contributions, avoiding unnecessarily offensive examples.  It is perfectly alright to raise questions that may seem to you to be unsophisticated or uninformed.  This is a place to learn and explore.  There are no right or wrong answers.

        Throughout my six year tenure at J.P. Morgan, these concepts; design thinking, positive deviance and lean start up, were foreign to our structured corporate environment. Unlike the three concepts, failure was generally not tolerated, there wasn’t a focus on simplification and the ideas did not directly focus on the consumer. With its’ focus on user’s experiences, simplification and toleration of failure, it aligns best with the technology firm I will be working for. Although I believe design thinking will work best for International Business Development I still have reservations about the strategy as a whole.                           Positive deviance suggests that the solution to the problem statement already exists within the community. Lean start-up suggests that the solution doesn't yet exist, but is discoverable through the community. Meanwhile, design thinking suggests that the solution doesn't already exist, nor is it even known by the community. Of the three, positive deviance is the most different. This is a matter of learning from the subject community, and is more a way of identifying and spreading ideas that are already in use and proven. The other two are all about discovery - you have to come into the problem solving without the constraints of what's already being done. In fact, your success in the latter two depends on your ability to think 'outside the box'. Design thinking is more creative - how can you converge around divergent possible solutions? While lean start-up is more responsive to discoveries that are made through customer interactions.  For my IBD project - helping college graduates turn their degree into a job - is one for which a lot of answers already exist (positive deviance). For example, we could look at career placement services at US universities (ie Berkeley), or African universities that might be more similar (ie University of Cape Town). However, even the latter might have minimal in common with the university we're working with in Uganda. Therefore, a different approach might be useful. Since we'll have limited opportunity to iterate with hypothesis in-country, the best approach for us might be design thinking. We can use our knowledge of existing solutions, and alternative placement functions, to think about how Makerere University might place its students.  Designing thinking is all about thinking outside of constraints. This is a helpful practice because we don't have a good sense of the constraints that actually exist. For example, does the University have limited financial resources available for hiring staff? I can see how our newness to the topic is a position of strength. However, I'm not sure at what point constraints should come into the discussion. It wouldn't be helpful, for example, for our solution to be an App, that costs multiple million dollars to develop, if the University isn't willing to commit that much.

                                The three methods, design thinking, positive deviance and lean start-up have a lot in common - the main similarity being their user-centric culture. But the mindsets and the applications are somewhat different. 1- User-centric: The three approaches are focused on users' experience and feedback. However, they use it in different ways. The design thinking approach builds products and value proposition that will have an emotional resonance with customers - utility and needs are important but the emotional value is paramount in this case. The lean startup approach establishes first a vision and then hypothesis that are tested with customers and either validated or rejected. The positive deviance relies on past experiences and identifies strategies that have worked in the past for a specific group of people to then encourage its replication to the rest of the community.  2- type of product / projects: The three strategies don't necessarily apply to the same projects or products. The design thinking approach is potentially more applicable to the design of complex new technologies and their integration to existing systems. Rather, the lean startup method is looking at improving a business model first. The positive deviance is more interested in practices and behaviors vs. actual products.  3-goals: Design thinking is looking at simplifying products while the lean startup model is about testing hypotheses on business models to end up with a model that avoids waste. Positive deviance is about sharing best practices.

                                                The lean start-up model is essentially a structured way to identify a problem, develop a hypothesis about that problem, test possible solutions, and respond to results. Beyond this structured framework to test and scale solutions is the underlying message that speaking with people in a variety of contexts, understanding their problems, and seeing how they react to solutions, is the best way to quickly develop effective new products. In this way, only by empathizing with one's customers -- putting yourself in their position -- can you really develop an innovative business.  Within the context of the lean startup model, design thinking can be viewed as a methodology to unpack complex problems, identify underlying causes, and hypothesize about possible solutions. Design thinking emphasizes the customer experience -- using empathy, like the lean start-up model -- to provide new insight into long-running problems. By using these "artifacts" and design-based methods, entrepreneurs can better understand customer needs and consider solutions. Positive deviance, similar to design thinking, is another method of identifying problems and creating possible solutions. Positive deviance also emphasizes empirical evidence, direct observation, and interviews with people -- however it is somewhat different in its approach. Rather than applying design models to think creatively about solutions, positive deviance encourages researchers to identify people who have fared better than their peers. Be systematically evaluating better performers we can then see if there are any viable trends that could be applied elsewhere. This is also related to the lean start-up idea of testing hypothesis -- see what is working in real life and then develop solutions that meet those real-world users. In relation to my IBD project, I think that using the lean start-up methodology to structure our overall efforts will be helpful, while using some of the techniques within design thinking and positive deviance can help us think creatively about how to solve our client's challenges and develop hypothesis for potential solutions. What all three of these ideas encourage is a user-centric, empathy-based model for identifying and solving customer challenges.

                                                                                “Lean Startup” is a specific process for developing and iterating a business model. This allows the entrepreneur to obtain valuable feedback quickly and assess whether they are actually solving for the right thing and whether potential customers would see their product as valuable. Design-thinking is a methodology used for problem-solving that emphasizes developing solutions and having a forward-thinking orientation (i.e. “exploring what could be”).  Design thinking is a helpful tool to employ during the lean startup process, as it allows you to obtain excellent raw data and information from your prospective customers.   The positive deviance approach to problem solving involves identifying actors within a community that have achieved an unexpectedly good outcomes despite being at high risk (for a given risk factor).                                                                                                         The key similarity between Design Thinking, Positive Deviance, and Lean Start-Up is that they all provide guidance on how to understand the problem from the customer's point of view. The difference lies in how each one of them achieves this goal. Design Thinking encourages organizations to think about customer's emotional resonance from value propositions instead of simply analyzing the utility of that proposition. Lean Start-Up emphasizes the need to develop "Minimum Viable Products" to test customer's likability of the product, and change the product based on these findings.                                                                                                                   Design thinking, positive deviance, and lean startup are frameworks and principles that fit well together in the context of a problem-solving approach, but there are some distinctions between them. Design thinking refers to an approach to a project that starts by exploring user needs by understanding their experiences and perspectives; then developing rapid prototypes to explore solutions that are then tested by users and iterated upon. The iterative nature of this process is essential to design thinking projects. Positive deviance refers to the pattern that in most settings, there are some individuals who carry out unusual behaviors that result in uncommon benefits to them compared to other community members who do not follow the behaviors. Typically this occurs when individuals face a high risk and develop a creative solution to their problem. The positive deviance approach involves partnering with members of the community practicing the “deviant” behavior to interview them to understand their practices and test whether they can be accessible and sustainably applied to others in the community, then aiming to replicate it on a broader scale. Although positive deviance approaches may involve some elements of design thinking, it is centered around the occurrence of unusually positive behaviors within a community setting. One way it is similar to design thinking is the focus on a deep exploration of the habits, behaviors, and motivations of people practicing positively deviant behavior, which relates to the interview phase of design thinking and requires a similar emphasis on empathy for your “user” or audience. Lean startup is similar to Design Thinking in that it also uses an iterative process of developing a prototype that is tested and iterated upon to improve the MVP. However, while Design Thinking results in the generation of a hypothesis, Lean Startup or hypothesis-centered entrepreneurship works to prove or disprove a hypothesis in the context of a startup business. Lean startup, like Design Thinking, requires flexibility and open-mindedness as the direction you begin going down may quickly change based on results. I worked in a startup that aimed to embody Lean Startup principles and used some Design Thinking strategies in our work with clients. We viewed each new program we launched as a prototype and continually iterated on our product as we gathered customer feedback, which included making several pivots in our product offering. In our work with clients, my boss focused a lot on empathy and understanding our customers’ experience using our technology. I would say that in IBD, empathy and the ability to deal with ambiguity and/or changing directions and pivoting, which are important principles of Design Thinking and Lean Startup, will be extremely important as we conduct interviews and formulate and test various hypotheses about the direction of our project. Questions for classmates - Positive deviance is the concept I have the least experience with and it seems intriguing; does anyone have experience with positive deviance in their work?
