    Module 1 Discussion: Innovation Processes - Hedge Fund Strategies

    From 
    Jan 22 at 3:57pm
    12 12 unread replies. 12 12 replies.

    Part of innovation is taking what you experience, including what you read, and thinking about the missing pieces, the next steps, or just something you are curious about. In each module of PFPS, you will do so with one or more of the readings from that week by considering the application of the concepts in the readings to your own life and work, capturing your thoughts on the Discussions pages in bCourses and responding to your classmates’ posts.

    This week’s introductory readings ask you to consider “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up” (aka hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship) frameworks, the basic mindsets and capabilities that underlie them and how they might play out in your lives or the organizations you work in.  As you read, think about the parallels and differences among the three approaches to innovation.

    Compose a thoughtful answer to the questions: what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”?  For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?  In what ways have the organizations you've worked in supported the development of these skills or mindsets? In what ways might they better support these approaches? Which approaches are likely to be most valuable in the Applied Innovation course you are taking?  What questions do you have about the approaches that you would like to discuss with your classmates?  Be specific in your responses.  

    Post your response, including questions for your classmates, on bCourses and then respond to at least two of your classmates.

    General Guidelines for Discussion: Be thoughtful, constructive and inclusive.  In your responses, be kind, present objections or different points of view open to the possibility that there may be a reasonable response, and object to the responses rather than to the people.  Objections are fine, but you can also choose to be constructive, and you needn't repeat the same objection multiple times.  Be inclusive in your contributions, acknowledging previous contributions, avoiding unnecessarily offensive examples.  It is perfectly alright to raise questions that may seem to you to be unsophisticated or uninformed.  This is a place to learn and explore.  There are no right or wrong answers.

        The three approaches to innovation all draw upon an underlying concept to identify solutions from unexpected places using social settings.  By that, I mean, these approaches all rely on external inputs to find solutions and involve the customer in the development process.   For example, in Design Thinking, the creators iterate rapidly with input from not only the internal development team, but the intended target.  Its a symbiotic relationship between the creator and the consumer.  Similarly, in the Lean Startup construct, the creator develops minimally viable products (MVP) and then tests them to answer a falsifiable hypothesis.  It requires analyzing consumer reactions to the MVP to guide the viability of the product and ultimately the enterprise.  Positive Deviance relies on finding solutions to problems by identifying the positive outliers in a societal group and then trying to mobilize groups to adopt the positive habits through a grassroots movement. However, there are many differences in how these techniques approach discovering and implementing innovative products/services/strategies.  The obvious deviant, is positive deviance.  This approach is focused on finding an already existing solution that is not commonly used and then trying to apply it to the broader community.  On the other hand Design Thinking and Lean Startup is about trying to mold a new solution to a need through iteration and a customer feedback loop.  Furthermore, we can differentiate Design Thinking and Lean Startup between an artistic and scientific approach.  Design Thinking seems to be a much more fluid, almost haphazard, approach while Lean Startup takes a scientific method to idea realization.  Design Thinking focuses on exploration and developing and modifying ideas rapidly throughout the process.  While on the other hand, Lean Startup takes a more measured, though still rapid prototyping, approach that requires much more analytical data-driven decision making.  Design Thinking is emotional, Lean Startup thoughtful. Coming from the military, innovative processes are neither encouraged or discouraged.  By-and-large, outside the box thinking is not highly encouraged.  However, I had the opportunity to work on a project in which we were given great latitude in how we approached the problem.  We quickly adopted a design thinking process and AGILE development methodology.  It was new to us and the organization.  While our group was tasked with finding a product solution, the process allowed us to discover a process solution that we felt could be repeated throughout the enterprise - namely using these innovation processes to drive change within the organization.  We found that there is support for innovative thinking and innovative techniques where mistakes can be tolerated.  However, there is a disconnect between senior leadership and lower leaders.  The military itself has a long tradition of intolerance of failure and divergent thinking.  These are major impediments to the innovative process and requires cultural change before these processes can realize their true benefits.  Senior leaders who already recognize the value of rapid innovation must establish a system for promoting the bedrock foundation - failure is an option, challenging ideas is a must - that facilitates adoption of innovative processes at lower levels of the organization. I believe for Hedge Fund Strategies, we can apply all three approaches.  Each have a place and I feel can be combined to in the correct way to be valuable.  For example, and this may seem out of place given the social construct within which Positive Deviance was formed, but the fundamental of Positive Deviance - identifying a cases that have achieved unexpected good outcomes - is core to the idea generation for the class where identifying outliers (either investment vehicles or strategies) that defy market efficiency to capitalize upon is paramount.  Then, developing falsifiable hypotheses for future trades and  rapid prototyping the ideas using Design Thinking balanced with backtesting may allow us to find new or less common strategies. As I come from a non-finance background, I am very curious to see how others who have worked within the field find these ideas applicable.  Our processes are important to applying your financial experience in new ways.  Are these processes really relevant?  Even if not at first apparently relevant, are there pieces that we as a group can borrow from each strategy and put together into our own framework that will facilitate our trade idea and strategy development?  Is it how we think about investment strategy that will differentiate the trade strategy - there are so many people already implementing strategies, how can we be different?  How can we use positive deviance (at a tactical level) to find what is working and then iterate those ideas into a new strategy?

            Hey Drew, I find your assertion that Design Thinking is emotional and Lean Startup is thoughtful resonates with me. To extend that even a little further, I view the two as opposing sides of the same coin in many ways. The Lean Startup uses tactical experimentation and measurement to innovate, working from the surface, observable data downward, while Design Thinking tries to understand the deep, emotional directions and drivers that users have, then makes meaningful connections with those drivers. To your point, Design Thinking does have a place in our strategy. The How Might We that we eventually decide to build our strategy around is pivotal to the definition of the strategy, the selection of the right assets, and proper balancing of risk against reward. The customer, who are often Institutional players in the case of hedge funds, have unique needs that should be at the center of our portfolio construction. On the point of Positive Deviance, I respectfully disagree. My perspective is that, from an investment standpoint, if you can see a contrarian opinion or tactic winning in the market, so can other investors. The flood of investors to this strategy normalizes returns, thereby forming an efficient market. On the point of customer experience however, perhaps there is an opportunity to find an area where Positive Deviance can be applied. Best, Nathan

                  In my perspective, one of the core aspects that seems to knit all three of these approaches to innovation is a robust analytical core that help to explain the forces at work which influence the system / problem in question that you are trying to address. In "design thinking", this mindset is require to analyze user's experiences and to link these experiences (both physical, logical and emotional) to a physical model which helps to define the variable attributes at work and to make ambiguity a little more tangible. For "positive defiance", the outlier approaches of individuals actively diverging from their peers must be isolated and assessed for their potential and the root cause of their success in order to be applied at a broader scale to the target population / application. For "lean start-up", hypothesis testing requires precise definition of variables and the overarching methodology in order to prove validity and avoid false positives. Where these methods differ most however, in my opinion, is in the level of comfort the innovator has with ambiguity. "Design thinking" often begins with the innovator having almost no idea of the end solution or even the variables at play that effect the user experience. "Positive defiance" does not require the innovator to create an idea as to the solution but rather to look at a finite number of outlier possibilities. Lastly, the "lean start-up" allows the innovator to create clear, intentional proposed hypotheses which they can then test and accept or reject with little ambiguity. At Deloitte, we differed in approaches. Doblin, which had a culture that allowed for free thinking and wandering without always knowing where you are headed, was conducive to "design thinking".  Deloitte Consulting on the other hand, liked measurable results, often had partners which preconceived ideas of the best solution and the ability to understand why hypotheses failed when they did. As such, this environment was better suited to "lean start-up". Though I have yet to discuss this with my classmates, I believe that the best approach to developing hedge fund strategies is by using "lean start-up". Repeatable strategies can be researched, developed, and tested using back-testing to determine MVP and validity with a large number of tools with large data sets available. Conversely, both "design thinking" and "positive defiance" do not fully apply to this problem. For "design thinking", there is no user whose experience needs to be defined. The closest parallel would likely be to look at other investors' sentiments and to build a behavioural finance strategy. For "positive defiance", contrarian thought is where abnormal returns come from. These defying opinions need to be independently created however, as there is a lack of transparency on other dissenting opinions in the market as well as their success.

                                One of the similarities that three approaches share is the use of prototyping. In “Design Thinking” there is a discipline of prototyping; In “Positive Deviance” a few at risk individuals that follow uncommon, beneficial practices and consequently experience better outcomes are researched as the prototype. In “Lean Start up” two concepts highly related to prototyping, which are MVP and pivoting, are introduced. The major difference among the three approaches is the extent to which risks are taken. In “Design Thinking” it is encouraged to accept more ambiguity and embrace risk; In “Positive Deviance” risk might be manipulated downward because the sample is from a specific group of people with beneficial practices and better outcomes and thus might produce unconvincing conclusions. In “Lean Start up” the major limitation is that the mistake should be limited. I used to work for a hedge fund where my main duty was equity research and portfolio management. The company uses design thinking a lot. For example, to find out and invest in companies which we call “with a solid fundamental”, we typically have a list of criterion (Prototype) to select a good company like sustainable growth rate, high margins, adequate leverage ratio, etc. The decision making process of buying/shorting a stock is based on known information combined with portfolio manager’s/analyst’s expectations as well as the probability that the expectation would be realized (accept more ambiguity/embrace risk). When holding the stock, portfolio managers/analysts might challenge themselves on all kinds of risks or concerns several times based on consecutive information acquired (reset expectations). Mistake is allowed, but in a managed way: position should be well diversified and discipline of cutting loss should be executed strictly (tolerance for failure).  Hedge funds should use the concept of “Pivoting” in “Lean Start up” to improve their trading strategies. Sometimes star portfolio managers would trust their gut or sense to manage the portfolio, but that would bring into disaster if situation loses control (think about the case in recent years that Bill Ackman invests in Valeant). Pivoting method in equity research would provide logic steps to find out the catalysts that trigger share price moving. The most valuable approach that I would learn from my Applied Innovation course, Hedge Fund Strategies, would probably be Lean Start Up. I like the idea of evaluating an early stage startup’s entire business model but not just products. For hedge fund people, it makes more sense to have a clearly defined strategy to convince investors before developing details for fund products. Also, I would love the idea of MVP test and the mindset of pivoting, which might be used to test new trading strategies.   The question about Positive Deviance that I would like to discuss with classmates is: In this approach,  what if there is a survivorship bias existing among those at risk individuals who follow uncommon, beneficial practices and experience better outcomes than neighbors? Also, how to make sure that this approach does not ignore other factors that affect the test results?
