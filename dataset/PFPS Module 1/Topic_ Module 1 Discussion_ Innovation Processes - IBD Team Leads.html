    Module 1 Discussion: Innovation Processes - IBD Team Leads

    From 
    Jan 22 at 3:57pm

    Part of innovation is taking what you experience, including what you read, and thinking about the missing pieces, the next steps, or just something you are curious about. In each module of PFPS, you will do so with one or more of the readings from that week by considering the application of the concepts in the readings to your own life and work, capturing your thoughts on the Discussions pages in bCourses and responding to your classmates’ posts.

    This week’s introductory readings ask you to consider “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up” (aka hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship) frameworks, the basic mindsets and capabilities that underlie them and how they might play out in your lives or the organizations you work in.  As you read, think about the parallels and differences among the three approaches to innovation.

    Compose a thoughtful answer to the questions: what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”?  For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?  In what ways have the organizations you've worked in supported the development of these skills or mindsets? In what ways might they better support these approaches? Which approaches are likely to be most valuable in the Applied Innovation course you are taking?  What questions do you have about the approaches that you would like to discuss with your classmates?  Be specific in your responses.  

    Post your response, including questions for your classmates, on bCourses and then respond to at least two of your classmates.

    General Guidelines for Discussion: Be thoughtful, constructive and inclusive.  In your responses, be kind, present objections or different points of view open to the possibility that there may be a reasonable response, and object to the responses rather than to the people.  Objections are fine, but you can also choose to be constructive, and you needn't repeat the same objection multiple times.  Be inclusive in your contributions, acknowledging previous contributions, avoiding unnecessarily offensive examples.  It is perfectly alright to raise questions that may seem to you to be unsophisticated or uninformed.  This is a place to learn and explore.  There are no right or wrong answers.

        There are a number of similarities between each of the three approaches discussed: careful observation and consideration of feedback, rapid iteration and implementation, and a deep connection with the user. Additionally, both design thinking and lean start-up find value in identifying failure as part of the innovation process. All three methods look to and interact with the world around them to develop new business models and affect change.  One key difference between these approaches is the source of the original idea. For example, with design thinking there is an extended ideation process that considers a wide range of ways to frame problems and solutions. Whereas with the lean start-up, the original hypothesis and vision comes from the founder or visionary and the subsequent testing then accepts or rejects the hypothesis. Finally, positive deviance focuses on careful observation to spot innovation.  As part of a consulting project for a car rental company, my team helped the client develop a new way of thinking about the customer experience by disregarding the existing processes/frameworks and approaching the car rental process from a blank slate. This allowed for truly innovative ideas that were not dictated by the current system. Unfortunately, creation of the IT infrastructure to execute on these ideas would take massive capital investment and a considerable amount of time. Nevertheless, it helped them develop a blueprint for their future business model.  On the other hand, working on short-term, fast-paced consulting projects typically did not allow for creative framing and reframing or prototyping and experimentation. Due to the timeline, we had to come to a recommendation quickly and efficiently and only had a few opportunities to iterate with the client. In many cases, the final product could have been more valuable had we had more time to break out of the typical case process and test more hypotheses.  I see two ways in which we can incorporate these approaches into our IBD projects. First, we can build team cultures that encourage design thinking or a lean start-up approach as we develop recommendations and deliverables for our clients. Additionally, our recommendation to the client can incorporate these methods where appropriate. For example, my client is interested in international expansion. As they enter new markets with different expectations and cultures, they will need to be open to experimentation and testing prior to full scaling.  Question for my peers:  In your past work/school experience, have you ever consciously tried to foster the mindset behind these approaches? How did your team respond? Was it effective?  What was challenging?

                        Module 1 Discussion: Innovation Processes - IBD Team Leads Among design thinking, positive deviance, and the lean startup approaches, there are a couple of key common elements: the need for observation and user empathy. For design thinking, one must understand what people want and need—and this data must be gathered through observation rather than speculation. Positive deviance interventions emerged as public health researchers closely observed at-risk individuals who were showing (unexpectedly) positive health outcomes. Through this observation, researchers were able to identify which behaviors others in the community should replicate. Importantly, community members would be more likely to adopt these behaviors because they would be culturally acceptable. Understanding patients’ mindsets and how they perceive new public health interventions—including bottom-up and top-down ones—is key to the positive deviance approach. While the lean startup approach has a less intensive focus of observation, this is still a key step in proving or disproving hypotheses through MVP tests. These concepts are relatively new to me. Before Haas I worked in U.S. education reform on both the policy and consulting side. I was initially drawn to policy because I believed that public policy change was the only way to improve education systems at scale. I worked at a social policy research firm for a couple of years where I carried out impact evaluations of education policies. In other words, I was carrying out experiments to test whether policies were working or not. We used hypothesis testing so that we could provide an objective analysis to policymakers, foundations, and school districts and help them decide which programs to invest in—and which ones were not worth their money or effort (even if they sounded good on paper). While we would sometimes gather information through interviews and focus groups, we were not focused on deeply understanding individuals or their experience. At the same time, I am struggling to understand how design thinking could have been applied to this type of work, and whether it would even be appropriate to try to do so. My firm’s objective was always to use quantitative data and randomized studies to measure policy impact—I’m not sure we would say that our value-add to the field was that we understood user experience (how a teacher perceived a specific policy, for instance). I suppose what I’m getting at is that I’m somewhat of a design thinking skeptic. While there are many things I love about the approach, I don’t know if I believe that it’s appropriate in certain situations or lines of work. I am curious to hear my classmates' perspectives on this.   On the other hand, I’ve also had a very positive experience with design thinking through my recent work with a very early-stage edtech startup. The startup has created a tool to help students develop a “growth mindset,” which is the belief that intelligence can be developed over time and that one can become smarter with effort and the right strategies. Our focus right now is on improving the software based on user feedback. To better understand how teachers are currently perceiving the product and sign-up process, I carried out a couple of usability studies with former teachers to observe them as they used the software. What was intuitive and what was confusing? What were they thinking when they clicked on a specific button? This type of observation was critical in helping to inform the next iteration of the product. For my IBD project, I believe that design thinking and the positive deviance approach will be most valuable. Right now it is hard for me to understand my client's mission and theory of change because I don't understand the experience of a college student in Uganda (far from it!). I'm excited to dig in through conversations with my client and interviews. The positive deviance approach could also be useful--where are we seeing "unexpectedly" good employment and life outcomes among college graduates who face the same challenges as their peers (i.e. a lack of alignment between what they're learning in college and what they need to know to be competitive in the workforce)?

                                                        Lean Startups require a more minimalist and step-by-step approach to growing your company. You can even “pretend” delivering the solution to customer problems while the engineers are busy building the product. In no means, this pretension will mean fooling the customer. In contrary, it is just testing to see if this product will solve the customer’s problems.                                                                                                                                                 What are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”? These three frameworks at their core, fundamentally agree: they emphasize the need to truly understand and be close to the customer. Specifically, understanding the customer enables one to identify positive outliers (positive deviance), design empathetically (design thinking), and rapidly prove/disprove hypotheses.                                                                                                                                                                       Design thinking would be most useful for our IBD team, as it would enable us to keep a keen eye on what the Thai consumer would be interested in. Positive deviance could also be helpful in identifying examples of real estate companies who had succeeded via unconventional methods. As we are gathering criteria for selecting strategic development strategies, we could adopt the lean startup methodology of requesting feedback from our client as each stage, so that we can validate our approach with them.

                                                                                                                                                                      Questions:

                                                                                                                                                                      To what extent should user empathy play a role in building out a lean startup model?

                                                                                                                                                                      Is it possible to harness positive deviance in a systematic way to generate interesting new hypotheses to test in the lean startup model?
