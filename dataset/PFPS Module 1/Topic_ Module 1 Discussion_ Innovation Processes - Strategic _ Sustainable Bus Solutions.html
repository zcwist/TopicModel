    Module 1 Discussion: Innovation Processes - Strategic & Sustainable Bus Solutions

    Part of innovation is taking what you experience, including what you read, and thinking about the missing pieces, the next steps, or just something you are curious about. In each module of PFPS, you will do so with one or more of the readings from that week by considering the application of the concepts in the readings to your own life and work, capturing your thoughts on the Discussions pages in bCourses and responding to your classmates’ posts.

    This week’s introductory readings ask you to consider “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up” (aka hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship) frameworks, the basic mindsets and capabilities that underlie them and how they might play out in your lives or the organizations you work in.  As you read, think about the parallels and differences among the three approaches to innovation.

    Compose a thoughtful answer to the questions: what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”?  For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?  In what ways have the organizations you've worked in supported the development of these skills or mindsets? In what ways might they better support these approaches? Which approaches are likely to be most valuable in the Applied Innovation course you are taking?  What questions do you have about the approaches that you would like to discuss with your classmates?  Be specific in your responses.  

    Post your response, including questions for your classmates, on bCourses and then respond to at least two of your classmates.

    General Guidelines for Discussion: Be thoughtful, constructive and inclusive.  In your responses, be kind, present objections or different points of view open to the possibility that there may be a reasonable response, and object to the responses rather than to the people.  Objections are fine, but you can also choose to be constructive, and you needn't repeat the same objection multiple times.  Be inclusive in your contributions, acknowledging previous contributions, avoiding unnecessarily offensive examples.  It is perfectly alright to raise questions that may seem to you to be unsophisticated or uninformed.  This is a place to learn and explore.  There are no right or wrong answers.

        “Design thinking”, “positive deviance” and “lean start-up” are all very people-focused and keep the end user in mind. They are all similar in process in that each requires a culture that is comfortable with failure. The systems embrace imperfection and approach problems in a way that builds in room for growth and evolution as well as the expectation that the best solution will likely be quite different than the initial vision of a solution. Conversely, design thinking and lean start-up have broader business applications than positive deviance. Positive deviance is based on finding a solution that is already in practice and encouraging wider adoption by a community by education and word-of-mouth. This contrasts with lean start-up and design thinking which are both more focused on innovating entirely new products or systems. I worked in a more traditional organization in the service industry where consistency, client service, and effectiveness were higher priorities than innovation. They tended to be more reactive than proactive. Although processes were built with the client in mind, I think they could benefit most from a design thinking approach where the customer experience is central to the decision-making process. I think that design thinking could also help them to adapt to changing needs of clients and trends in the industry.

                    Design thinking, positive deviance, and lean start-up are all effective approaches to driving change. They are similar in that they provide structure to innovation, a concept many people imagine is a mental black box. Design thinking is particularly customer-centric, while positive deviance relies heavily on observation, and lean start-up focuses primarily on the development process. Each have their benefits: design thinking is useful for framing a problem, or identifying customer needs; positive deviance can provide examples of effective change agents; lean start-up is feedback intensive, refining an idea based on evidence. Design thinking requires empathy, hence its effectiveness at identifying customer needs. Positive deviance requires an initial deviant, which can be limiting when facing wicked problems. Lean start-up requires an initial idea or innovation, which can call for a certain level of creativity.   When I worked at Axiom EPM, a software firm, we were constantly launching new features and testing them with clients. The organization relied heavily on this lean start-up approach, but with a healthy dose of design-thinking. The consultants would meet weekly with the development team to discuss challenges and opportunities witnessed from the field, helping to drive changes as the product evolved to meet our customers' needs. The challenge came when we realized that the product overall felt clunky and didn't have the best UI. We stopped releasing updates (other than bug fixes) for a year, and spent that time developing a new product, moving from a numbered release system to a named product, a la Apple. This allowed the team to step back and create an overhaul that couldn't be broken up into piecemeal. The consultants were able to test components of the system and ensure that the product was headed in the right direction, but since the actual functionality of the software wasn't changing very much, the main component being tested was the visual design and the navigation. This organization did a great job of seeking to understand customers' needs, but the main drawback was that change was expensive and resource-intensive.   I think design thinking will be the most useful for our team. Patagonia aims to be a positive deviant, so looking for an example might not help guide us in the right direction. Since our project is primarily marketing and communications-centered, we need to be extremely empathetic to customers, and seek to understand what they want and need from Patagonia's messaging. Our assignment is a little vague: we need to develop a marketing plan to communicate Patagonia's CSR initiatives. We haven't been told "why" this needs to happen, yet, so framing the problem is going to be critical.   My questions: How could we employee lean start-up in a marketing context? Specifically will we be able to test out specific components of a marketing plan? Are there any positive deviants that can inspire us without leading us to mimicry?

                                  As technology becomes increasingly complex and more difficult to use, it has become crucial that tech innovators work consciously toward making interactions with technology to be simple, intuitive, and pleasurable. This has increased the popularity of design thinking, a way of approaching a problem that is based on empathy with users, a discipline of protoyping, and tolerance for failure. Accordingly, the best design thinkers are observant, creative, risk-tolerant, and flexible. On the innovation cycle matrix, design thinking falls under the “imagine and design” category, combining abstract conceptualism and how. Positive deviance is a way of approaching problems that learns by observing successful existing practices. The three important processes involved are social mobilization, information fathering to craft interventions, and behavior change. Accordingly, individuals who are observant and research-oriented are best suited for positive deviance. On the innovation cycle matrix, it falls under the “observe and notice” category, combining concrete experience and why. Lean start-up, by my evaluation, stands in the middle of design thinking and positive deviance, because it falls under the “make and experiment” quadrant of the innovation cycle matrix, sharing how with design thinking and concrete experiment with positive deviance. It encourages minimizing waste and presenting minimum viable products (MVP) to its customers to learn from consumers what works and doesn’t work. Accordingly, it values action-oriented, observant thinkers. At Melissa & Doug, an educational toy company, product development most utilized lean start-up by protoyping many iterations of products using minimal resources. PD was effective with this approach. In sales (my function), we most utilized positive deviance, learning from what others in the industry were doing to succeed. We could have incorporated more of a design thinking approach, taking a fresh look at what would be ideal, versus what we had been stuck doing. Too often, we were stuck in the weeds of our own approaches. I would argue that design thinking will be most crucial to Strategic & Sustainable Business Solutions. The companies for whom we’re consulting are already, at least in some form, sustainability-oriented leaders, so while learning from their peers a la positive deviance has some value, it's not forward-thinking enough. Employing design thinking would allow these leaders to picture their ideal scenario and seek to reach it. Similarly, iterating and reiterating a la lean start-up may have value, but because in most cases, we’re not producing a tangible product, lean start-up may not have as much relevance. For my project, Levi's, specifically, I think that employing a positive deviance approach would be most effective. Introducing a carbon tax is not something that should be iterated and reiterated. I believe this would create confusion for users. I also don't believe design thinking would be effective, because there seems to be too many potential pitfalls to go from 0 to the ideal scenario. Observing others approaches with positive deviance would reveal qualities that made others successful. I would like to discuss the employment of these three technologies with my classmates with consulting backgrounds. I understand that in the past several years, design thinking has become far more prevalent in the industry, and I am curious to what extent this has signaled a true shift in the fundamentals of a consultant’s approach to solving problems.

                                                Design thinking, Lean Start-Up, and Positive deviance are all approaches to quickly test solutions or innovative approaches to existing issues and needs. The process also relies heavily on prototypes, therefore investing more upfront than the Lean Start-Up process. Finally, it emphasizes a need for creativity. The Positive Deviance approach presents the greatest difference. It focuses on identifying solutions and replicating them in new areas, or scaling them to additional segments. By doing a deep dive into alternative, rather than mainstream solutions, the approach surfaces new types of innovation.

Rather than trying to develop a hypothesis, the design thinking approach focuses on observing behavior to focus on experience. The process also relies heavily on prototypes, therefore investing more upfront than the Lean Start-Up process. Finally, it emphasizes a need for creativity.

                                                The Positive Deviance approach presents the greatest difference. It focuses on identifying solutions and replicating them in new areas, or scaling them to additional segments. By doing a deep dive into alternative, rather than mainstream solutions, the approach surfaces new types of innovation.

                                                      One similarity between design thinking, positive deviance, and lean start-up is they are all ways of solving a problem. One difference is the type of problem being solved. Design thinking is a way of solving the following problem: what is the optimal way users could experience this service or thing. Positive deviance is a way of solving the following problem: how do you identify and make more common the successful behaviors of a few individuals in an at-risk community. Lean start-up is a way of solving the following problem: should you launch your start-up and, if so, what should be your business model. All three require a lot of patience. Design thinking and lean start-up require creativity as it is you who is coming up with a solution versus finding those who have already come up with a successful trait or action (positive deviance). My first job was at Bain & Co. Bain teams operate in a way very similar to lean start-up. They are presented with a problem. They do “answer first,” which is their way of saying coming up with a hypothesis right off the bat. They then figure out the minimum tests that need to be done to falsify that hypothesis. If passed, they do more tests to strengthen and refine that hypothesis. If failed, a new hypothesis would be created and the process would begin again. My last job was at Revolution Foods. I think the organization should have employed positive deviance a lot more to allow more skill and knowledge sharing. For example, there was wide variance in how people managed their inbox and work to list management. There should have been an effort to see the wide array of ways people were going about these tasks and then disseminating the most effective ways to everyone. I believe design thinking will be most valuable for my Applied Innovation course. Patagonia believes it can do an even better job communicating its sustainability work to its customers. Our work has been identifying what are the biggest hurdles to that information currently and designing and testing new ways of accessing that information that are easier for customers. One question I have for my classmates is the following. Is it correct to thinking that design thinking could be put under lean start-up? I.e. what is the difference between a series of MVP tests and the iteration of design thinking?
