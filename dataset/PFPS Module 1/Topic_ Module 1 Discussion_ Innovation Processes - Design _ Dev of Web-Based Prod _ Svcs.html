    Module 1 Discussion: Innovation Processes - Design & Dev of Web-Based Prod & Svcs

    From 
    Jan 22 at 3:57pm
    25 25 unread replies. 25 25 replies.

    Part of innovation is taking what you experience, including what you read, and thinking about the missing pieces, the next steps, or just something you are curious about. In each module of PFPS, you will do so with one or more of the readings from that week by considering the application of the concepts in the readings to your own life and work, capturing your thoughts on the Discussions pages in bCourses and responding to your classmates’ posts.

    This week’s introductory readings ask you to consider “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up” (aka hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship) frameworks, the basic mindsets and capabilities that underlie them and how they might play out in your lives or the organizations you work in.  As you read, think about the parallels and differences among the three approaches to innovation.

    Compose a thoughtful answer to the questions: what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”?  For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?  In what ways have the organizations you've worked in supported the development of these skills or mindsets? In what ways might they better support these approaches? Which approaches are likely to be most valuable in the Applied Innovation course you are taking?  What questions do you have about the approaches that you would like to discuss with your classmates?  Be specific in your responses.  

    Post your response, including questions for your classmates, on bCourses and then respond to at least two of your classmates.

    General Guidelines for Discussion: Be thoughtful, constructive and inclusive.  In your responses, be kind, present objections or different points of view open to the possibility that there may be a reasonable response, and object to the responses rather than to the people.  Objections are fine, but you can also choose to be constructive, and you needn't repeat the same objection multiple times.  Be inclusive in your contributions, acknowledging previous contributions, avoiding unnecessarily offensive examples.  It is perfectly alright to raise questions that may seem to you to be unsophisticated or uninformed.  This is a place to learn and explore.  There are no right or wrong answers.

        In my opinion, the most important feature which is shared by design-thinking, positive deviance and lean start-ups is the emphasis put on customers’ insight: Design-thinking presupposes to use design artefact and prototypes either to get a better understanding of consumers’ behavior or to test a solution to a consumers’ problem, Positive deviance implies to carefully analyze a population to detect unusual behavior that would have a positive impact on solving an issue, Finally, lean start-ups use experimentations to help them move forward into their designing and creative endeavors. All of these approaches place consumers’ observation is core.   The second conclusion that can be drawn from those three articles is the importance of focusing on simple and necessary features. These sould resonate with as many individuals in the target population as possible. Positive deviance recommends to start with small things that can have a huge impact (cleaning a blade to cut a newborn cord). Both design-thinking and hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship focus on products whose features are limited to what is needed to consumers rather than what “would-be-nice”. This second feature is extremely important, since I have measured how, in product development, focusing on certain consumers’ demands can (i) drive you away from your main purpose, (ii) raise development costs and (iii) alienate another part of your customer base. Indeed, my company was developing financial products to be sold to insurance companies. At that time, new regulation were being enforced in European financial system, requiring to densify risk analyses. Some of our customers started to ask us for outsourcing risk calculations. To satisfy these demands, my CEO decided that we should systematically implement those calculations into our models, making them considerably more complex and hard to manipulate. Finally, we got back from this decision since our customers were not able to make an investment decision, facing too many complexities.   These various methods also present some differences, though. Among them, the context in which to apply these strategies and the final purpose they serve differ greatly. Positive deviance seems to focus on solving problem for the underprivileged, lean startups is well-designed for companies intending to launch a new product into the market. Finally, design-thinking is a mindset that should be adopted whatever the professional context.   Questions This being said, one could alleged that this contextual difference is not completely accurate. For instance, why should not we apply positive deviance to business issues? I wonder whether Airbus, inspiring itself from shark’s skins to reduce its planes’ gas consumption can be considered a form of positive deviance (by noticing how more efficient sharks were compared to other species). If so, wouldn’t it be right to recommend, when feasible, the use of positive deviance as a first step in the creative process. After this first-round, companies should use hypothesis-driven to refine their idea and instill a design-thinking mindset to enhance corporate creativity.

        In my opinion, the most important feature which is shared by design-thinking, positive deviance and lean start-ups is the emphasis put on customers’ insight:

        • Design-thinking presupposes to use design artefact and prototypes either to get a better understanding of consumers’ behavior or to test a solution to a consumers’ problem,
        • Positive deviance implies to carefully analyze a population to detect unusual behavior that would have a positive impact on solving an issue,
        • Finally, lean start-ups use experimentations to help them move forward into their designing and creative endeavors.

        All of these approaches place consumers’ observation is core.

         

        The second conclusion that can be drawn from those three articles is the importance of focusing on simple and necessary features. These sould resonate with as many individuals in the target population as possible. Positive deviance recommends to start with small things that can have a huge impact (cleaning a blade to cut a newborn cord). Both design-thinking and hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship focus on products whose features are limited to what is needed to consumers rather than what “would-be-nice”.

        This second feature is extremely important, since I have measured how, in product development, focusing on certain consumers’ demands can (i) drive you away from your main purpose, (ii) raise development costs and (iii) alienate another part of your customer base. Indeed, my company was developing financial products to be sold to insurance companies. At that time, new regulation were being enforced in European financial system, requiring to densify risk analyses. Some of our customers started to ask us for outsourcing risk calculations. To satisfy these demands, my CEO decided that we should systematically implement those calculations into our models, making them considerably more complex and hard to manipulate. Finally, we got back from this decision since our customers were not able to make an investment decision, facing too many complexities.

         

        These various methods also present some differences, though. Among them, the context in which to apply these strategies and the final purpose they serve differ greatly. Positive deviance seems to focus on solving problem for the underprivileged, lean startups is well-designed for companies intending to launch a new product into the market. Finally, design-thinking is a mindset that should be adopted whatever the professional context.

         

        Questions

        This being said, one could alleged that this contextual difference is not completely accurate. For instance, why should not we apply positive deviance to business issues? I wonder whether Airbus, inspiring itself from shark’s skins to reduce its planes’ gas consumption can be considered a form of positive deviance (by noticing how more efficient sharks were compared to other species). If so, wouldn’t it be right to recommend, when feasible, the use of positive deviance as a first step in the creative process. After this first-round, companies should use hypothesis-driven to refine their idea and instill a design-thinking mindset to enhance corporate creativity.

                    Beat Wuffli

                    Feb 2, 2017 Feb 2 at 3:24pm
                    Taking apart this question, my first intuition is to focus on what part or which steps of the process each "innovation methodology" focuses on. For the sake of this consideration, let us consider a simplification of innovation process as a simple three-step process Input (Ideation) --> Processing (Concretization / Execution) --> Output (Evaluation / Result). Furthermore, we acknowledge that this process is an iterative one with one period's outputs serving as (partial) inputs for the next round. From this perspective, both "design thinking" and "lean start-up" appear as philosophies covering the entire process. However, "design thinking" seems to have the strongest relevance in the first half of the process i.e. in the iterative creation of ideas and early concretization. Meanwhile, as we approach the final step, there is still a need to translate the results of "design thinking" into real-world services that can be applied to the creation of actual services and products. "Lean start-up" with its origins in lean manufacturing is clearly loaded towards the process step. It provides an approach to concretize these steps while reducing risk and costs as much as possible. Finally, "positive deviance", starts at the end of the process already with a successful result respectively with discovering outliers to serve as inspiration. In many ways in this discussion, it appears to be the odd one in this trinity. Both "design thinking" and "lean start-up" share a certain focus on front-loading creation of insights and reducing the amount of resources required to reach insights. "Lean start-up" methodology's take on this is to elevate the running of experiments based on hypotheses to be tested to the highest position. The results of experiments - the realistic the better, but at the lowest possible cost - are evaluated quantitatively and serve as the definition of goodness in this system. “Positive deviance” on the other hand appears to be a traditional improvement / optimization strategy. In other words, one gets the impression that "positive deviance" is just another way of saying "look at who / what is doing great - then try to understand why - and try to replicate it". This approach therefore is more of an outlier discovery and benchmarking exercise rather than the creation of anything new. In other words, this approach presupposes the existence of success stories. On its own, it does not help with the generation of new solutions - which is not to say that finding existing solutions and applying them broadly cannot generate a great amount of value. It is telling that this approach is presented in the context of a case study in (public sector) development aid - a sector with traditionally strong and inflexible bureaucracies, top-down decision-making, high change resistance and low competition. In functioning private market sectors, this kind of adapt and imitate to survive and thrive approach is almost a necessity for long-term survival in any industry. Another real-world consideration is the adoption of which of these approaches will cause the least disruption within the operations of an existing organization. Starting with "positive deviance" where in my opinion lack of its presence in the thinking approach "what worked, can we get more of it" on its own would be a powerful symptom of organizational dysfunctionality. "Lean start-up" methodology strikes me as somewhere on a continuum from mildly disruptive to severely disruptive. On one end of the spectrum is structuring specific (usually small) high-risk projects in a clearly experimental way. The only disadvantage that comes to mind is that within corporations - for various obvious reasons, the incentives against being in charge of a failed project are significant. Inevitably, the consequences of failure associated with a project - no matter the benefit of killing it early - have a tendency to become associated with the person leading the effort. Likewise, "design thinking" can also be implemented along an entire spectrum from completely harmless to medium disruptive. An entirely harmless approach would be to simply run "design thinking" seminars with teammates often with an outside facilitator. Frankly, I feel that in many industries, these "workshops" - while fun and excellent team-building exercises, rarely have a significant impact on operations. Would you agree with my critical interpretation of "positive deviance" and questioning of its inclusion as one of the key innovation processes? How do you judge the implement ability of each of the three approaches in the workplace?

                                          Feb 3, 2017 Feb 3 at 5:36am
                                          what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”? For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different? To start with lean start-up, it is a method to effectively construct, validate and realize a whole business model, based on hypothesizing, testing and pivoting on every single element of the model. Design thinking is a process to effectively understand and come up with solutions for complex issues, not limited to business model, through observation and experiment with emphasis on subjects' emotional elements. Positive deviance is an approach to find out powerful solutions within a inner group of people having the same issues, aiming at smooth and more effective implementation to the rest of the group. Emphasis on observation of subjects, rather than sticking to implementer's belief or vision, is a shared value across these approaches. In other words, these methods aim at learning from the real people that they cater to. Given necessary interaction with real people, strong interpersonal skills, especially ones to let people talk and express their emotions, are required. Also, ability to understand qualitative data is necessary. Adding to importance of observation, tolerance for failure is also a core principle for lean start-up and design thinking (though it is not clear enough from the reading, this may not apply to Positive deviance). As illustrated in MIT's "Demo or die" example, no single hypothesis or idea could be effective enough unless it is tested by the real people.                                           what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”? For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?

                                          To start with lean start-up, it is a method to effectively construct, validate and realize a whole business model, based on hypothesizing, testing and pivoting on every single element of the model. Design thinking is a process to effectively understand and come up with solutions for complex issues, not limited to business model, through observation and experiment with emphasis on subjects' emotional elements. Positive deviance is an approach to find out powerful solutions within a inner group of people having the same issues, aiming at smooth and more effective implementation to the rest of the group.

                                          Emphasis on observation of subjects, rather than sticking to implementer's belief or vision, is a shared value across these approaches. In other words, these methods aim at learning from the real people that they cater to. Given necessary interaction with real people, strong interpersonal skills, especially ones to let people talk and express their emotions, are required. Also, ability to understand qualitative data is necessary.

                                          Adding to importance of observation, tolerance for failure is also a core principle for lean start-up and design thinking (though it is not clear enough from the reading, this may not apply to Positive deviance). As illustrated in MIT's "Demo or die" example, no single hypothesis or idea could be effective enough unless it is tested by the real people. Obsession with specific ideas is an obstacle for these approaches.

                                          They differ in the areas of interest they intend to cover. Design thinking handles any kind of issues, and it provides guidelines both for how to identify the problem and resolve it. Positive deviance is also specific, while its practical use could be vast, as it deals with solutions rather than problems.                                                                         The three concepts “design thinking,” “positive deviance” and “lean start-up” share many similarities. They are all organized processes that ultimately promote an efficient and measurable outcome. They all require following a defined step-by-step approach that usually involves a definition phase, research phase, analysis phase, and then evaluation phase. However, one clear difference is in the outcome expected out of the three processes. For design thinking, the process is usually in pursuit of designing a new product or idea in the best way possible. For lean start-up, the process is usually in pursuit of identifying a solution to a problem or “job to be solved.” For positive deviance, the process is to identify best-in-class performers and identify what we can learn and apply across other use cases.             The organizations I’ve worked with in the past tended to focus more on “design thinking” and “positive deviance.” We used design thinking more often than lean start-up because as a brand strategy consulting firm, our clients usually came to us with an existing product or an idea of what product they want to launch already. So rather than start from “job to be done” phase, we usually start with tackling the question, "given an existing product idea for launch, how do we better design it to meet customer needs and ensure it will deliver?" We use positive deviance often since this process looks at past performers and identifies successful behaviors. Given we were advising clients, this was a commonly used method since we could refer to our client’s past actions and/or other successful companies and what they have accomplished.             My question is, do organizations need to utilize all three of these approaches? Are there certain approaches better suited for certain types of organizations? Or should teams be trained in using all three, and know what sort of problems they can tackle with each approach? I tend to favor simplicity, so I think teaching three different yet overlapping approaches can cause unnecessary confusion. Instead, I propose learning how one overall ‘process’ works, and understanding how to tweak certain stages of the process depending on the type of problem one is trying to solve.
