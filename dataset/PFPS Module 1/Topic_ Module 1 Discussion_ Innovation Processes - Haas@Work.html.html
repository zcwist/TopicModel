    Module 1 Discussion: Innovation Processes - Haas@Work

    From 
    Jan 22 at 3:57pm
    21 21 unread replies. 21 21 replies.

    Part of innovation is taking what you experience, including what you read, and thinking about the missing pieces, the next steps, or just something you are curious about. In each module of PFPS, you will do so with one or more of the readings from that week by considering the application of the concepts in the readings to your own life and work, capturing your thoughts on the Discussions pages in bCourses and responding to your classmates’ posts.

    This week’s introductory readings ask you to consider “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up” (aka hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship) frameworks, the basic mindsets and capabilities that underlie them and how they might play out in your lives or the organizations you work in.  As you read, think about the parallels and differences among the three approaches to innovation.

    Compose a thoughtful answer to the questions: what are the similarities and differences among “design thinking”, "positive deviance" and “lean start-up”?  For example, how are the personal skills or mindsets required by each similar or different?  In what ways have the organizations you've worked in supported the development of these skills or mindsets? In what ways might they better support these approaches? Which approaches are likely to be most valuable in the Applied Innovation course you are taking?  What questions do you have about the approaches that you would like to discuss with your classmates?  Be specific in your responses.  

    Post your response, including questions for your classmates, on bCourses and then respond to at least two of your classmates.

    General Guidelines for Discussion: Be thoughtful, constructive and inclusive.  In your responses, be kind, present objections or different points of view open to the possibility that there may be a reasonable response, and object to the responses rather than to the people.  Objections are fine, but you can also choose to be constructive, and you needn't repeat the same objection multiple times.  Be inclusive in your contributions, acknowledging previous contributions, avoiding unnecessarily offensive examples.  It is perfectly alright to raise questions that may seem to you to be unsophisticated or uninformed.  This is a place to learn and explore.  There are no right or wrong answers.

        Design Thinking is basically the ability to think different, to have an imaginative leap into the desired future. In this process, one imagines a desired goal and then work backward; what has to be done today, tomorrow, and a year from now to achieve the desired goal. Design Thinking objective is to simplify complex systems, products or organizations and make them more intuitive and enjoyable. In a hypothesis driven or lean start-up, the entrepreneur or individual comes up with hypotheses to solve the problems or issue. Then tests each hypothesis with MVPs. From the feedback gather through these tests, the entrepreneurs either modify the product, drop the product or just disregard the feedback and proceed with the original version. This approach is a valuable strategy for people and organization with limited resources. MVPs can be built with very simple and inexpensive materials, which then can be tested many times until the final desired product is created. Hypothesis should be measurable and falsifiable. Otherwise, there won’t be any learning. Positive Deviance basically says that sometimes some individuals take unusual approaches to a certain problem and achieve better results than their peers. Positive Deviance tend to happen in less complex communities or countries. This approach taps into the collective intelligence of a society to deliver a positive social change. All three have at least one thing in common, approaching a problem from a different perspective. Now, whether this approach or thinking is deliberate or arbitrary, is a different question. The type of mindset required varies across the three. A Positive Deviant may achieve the desired results without much thinking, per se. He or she, just like the mothers in Egypt, have relied on the uncommon traditions and achieved better results. The results can then be used to deliver a much broader social good in the community. On the other hand, in Lean Start-up, deliberate planning takes place, which encompasses everything from the vision all the way to the product-market fit. In this process, it’s imperative that the entrepreneur is open-minded and willing to accept failure. Otherwise, there is no benefit in testing or coming up with MVP. A judge-free zone and the ability to think out of the box makes Design Thinking different from the other two approaches. A problem or issue gets framed in many different ways until an exhaustive list of ideas are generated, and of course there is no right or wrong way. The industries I worked for barely applied any of these approaches. Heavy manufacturing (steel and bearings) didn’t believe in Design Thinking. The product was heavy, ugly steel. I believe at least one of the companies, a century old manufacturing, could have applied the Design Thinking approach to many of the problems it tried to solve. Just like GE has been doing lately. Currently, in Haas@Work class, we are using Design Thinking approach. It allows us to have an imaginative leap in to the future, and picture the desired objective that we are trying to achieve. It will be interesting to see the if the process 'really' works.  Questions: What are some ways to persuade a boss or an organization to believe in the value of Design Thinking approach? Is the act of Positive Deviance deliberate or arbitrary? Does thinking go into play before one applies the uncommon strategies to solve the problem?

                      I see design thinking is a process to make sense of complex problems by empathizing and seeing problems from the lens of users to come up with imaginative solutions, which will then be tested and reiterated to effectively address the problem. Unlike traditional problem-solving, design thinking does not draw solutions from existing best practices or past scientific research. Design thinking always starts with understanding the problems through user empathy. Similarly, lean start-up approach starts with understanding customers prior to developing falsifiable hypothesis and testing it through minimum viable prior (MVP) before making the decision to 1) reiterate the idea (so-called pivot), 2) move on to the new idea (so-called perish), or 3) pursue the idea (so-called scale). In fact, I believe design thinking is part of lean start-up process, which solves the problem from fresh perspectives. Since both processes are somewhat ambiguous, having an open mindset to embrace uncertainty and failure (from hypothesis testing) is essential. On the other hand, positive deviance relies on data mining to identify existing best practices from the uncommon case among similar demographic that yields better outcome. Thus, positive deviance strategy would only work if the assumption that there is already best practice that works for and accessible to the rest of the demographic that shares the same problem holds true. Similar to design thinking and lean start-up, positive deviance requires practitioners to empathize with users. However, the process to discover the uncommon positive case can be challenging, which is usually “at a prevalence of 1-10%.” In terms of the application to Haas@Work project, my team has to solve the longstanding problem in the ticketing industry, which involves unsold tickets or empty seats in sold-out venues/events. According to the clients, every ticketing company including StubHub has tried to address the problem whether through ticket pricing recommendation mechanism or location-based event recommendation. However, no one in the industry has figured out it yet. In this case, I believe we can utilize design thinking to come up with new solutions by understanding the situation from event goers’ perspectives and use positive deviance to learn best practices from other industries with ticketing mechanism such as airline industry.

                                                  In my opinion, the main similarity among design thinking, positive deviance and lean startup is that they all provide a hands-on approach to problem solving. The idea behind design thinking is that in order to be able to make sense of the complex world in which we live in, we need to create models to understand the problems we are trying to solve and then prototype solutions. Positive deviance also tries to solve complex problems through experimentation, but the focus is not on modeling or prototyping but on identifying individuals that achieve better outcomes than their peers and enabling communities to adopt the behaviors that explain the improved outcomes as a way to improve the community’s overall wellbeing. Similar to design thinking, lean startups follow a hypothesis-driven approach to test and validate their business models and iterate through several minimum viable products.   Even though the three processes focus on iteration as a means to get to a solution, in the case of design thinking and lean startups the iteration is led by the people that are designing the prototypes / MVPs whereas in positive deviance the source of iteration is the natural variances in people’s lifestyles and behaviors. Therefore, design thinking and lean startups provide a more rigorous and intentional approach to hypothesis-driven problem solving than positive deviance.   Before coming to Haas, I was working at a big CPG company, that had a more traditional and less hypothesis-driven approach to problem solving than the ones we are studying in this class. Most big corporations have a very complex system of procedures and bureaucracy in place that makes it difficult for them to be lean and allow for non-linear processes and thoughts. In the past 2-3 years I saw a tendency in my company to focus more on the consumer experience and a shift towards providing services instead of only products. The idea behind design thinking is that in order to be able to make sense of the complex world in which we live in, we need to create models to understand the problems we are trying to solve and then prototype solutions. Positive deviance also tries to solve complex problems through experimentation, but the focus is not on modeling or prototyping but on identifying individuals that achieve better outcomes than their peers and enabling communities to adopt the behaviors that explain the improved outcomes as a way to improve the community’s overall wellbeing. Similar to design thinking, lean startups follow a hypothesis-driven approach to test and validate their business models and iterate through several minimum viable products.

                                                   

                                                  Even though the three processes focus on iteration as a means to get to a solution, in the case of design thinking and lean startups the iteration is led by the people that are designing the prototypes / MVPs whereas in positive deviance the source of iteration is the natural variances in people’s lifestyles and behaviors. Therefore, design thinking and lean startups provide a more rigorous and intentional approach to hypothesis-driven problem solving than positive deviance.

                                                                The Marine Corps spends a great deal of time focused on discerning the benefit or impact of a proposed venture on the end user, the Marine.  While I was never directly involved with developing products, equipment, or tools to be used, I was often put in charge of selecting from a list the most appropriate and useful items that my Marines ought to carry.  In a sense, I was involved with maximizing the utility and efficacy of our products.  In this, there was a great deal of focus on the impact of these decisions on the end user.  Would the pack be too heavy for the mileage? Did the gear sufficiently protect them? Will they be able to carry enough water?  In some cases of paused deliberation between two sets of gear, we would build each proposed equipment list and then explore their pros and cons before making a decision.  In this way, there were some similarities to design thinking, though also to waterfall planning!  Conversely, there were many instances and examples in which design thinking does not seem an appropriate structure. The most basic underlying similarity of design thinking, positive deviance, and the lean startup model is that they are all frameworks to approach solving complex problems.  Beyond that, they seem to mostly differ in their approaches and philosophies.  In my opinion, they seem to be more useful in certain applications than in others.  For example, positive deviance works great when dealing with sizeable populations or communities where outliers and hidden variables become less relevant.  The lean startup model only requires a community big enough to test the MVPs on, allowing for customer feedback.  In the hypothesis-driven approach, it seems as though the biggest barrier to innovating is the first step: “develop a vision.”  In contrast, design thinking begins by empathizing with the end product user, or even more broadly by observing the “problem” generally.  In my opinion, the difficulty in design thinking is when you must converge into a single, actionable solution.  It seems as though the scope of your solution is only as specific as the focus of the problem.  In my project at Haas@Work, I believe the design thinking method to be the most applicable approach, as this is a highly complex problem for which our client has already allocated considerable resources and time.  I am excited by our converging and diverging approach, which I think will allow us a chance that they haven’t had: to really broaden our efforts in order to find new solutions.  The risk is that this space is highly competitive and has seen substantial innovation.  I am curious whether design thinking has a limit, or a point of decreasing marginal returns!

                                                                        Any strategic course of action requires that we consider options and choose the option that best meets our needs. Design thinking, positive deviance, and lean start-up all require a mindset in which one is willing to consider options that are unorthodox and  is willing to choose options that are unproven. These three terms do not contradict each other, rather, each complements the other, emphasizing a different part of the option-to-decision value chain. Design thinking places most emphasizes the original insight collection and creative solution generation phases of work. Positive deviance most emphasizes unconventional paths to success, and ask us to draw similarities between our own situation and other mavericks. Lean start-up emphasis rapid prototyping and learning, with the core belief being that we cannot know what works until we try it.                My consulting work to-date has been hypothesis driven and risk adverse. We would consider options, look at successful comparison companies, and do some testing of our results – but each of these phases in the process was rather narrowly linked to what had already been done in another context before. Rarely would we step into truly new territory. In the Haas@work project we are working on I imagine we will cover all aspects of this work, in particular the design thinking / original idea generation. The idea that I want to explore over the coming year is how to we integrate expertise? i.e. if we know there are certain things you absolutely have to know, even if they have been tried before, how do you make sure that knowledge is used without constraining thinking. Is it possible to have the best of both worlds – a fresh mindset and a an experienced knowledge base.
